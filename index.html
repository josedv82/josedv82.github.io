Born in Spain, I have a formal education in High Performance and Sport Science. I began my career at youth basketball academies, often working with fewer resources, then expanded my experience across Europe and China. After spending more than six years with the Houston Astros in MLB, I now lead performance and R&D at BCSG, a multi-club sport firm managing soccer teams across international leagues. Having worked in basketball, baseball, and football (soccer), I’ve developed a strong interest and skills in data analysis and programming along the way. I'm passionate about building and consider myself an amateur entrepreneur.



Thoughts

A Personal Take on Various Aspects of High-Performance Environments. My Perspective, Not Universal Truth.

✖
  • Master the basics—they're 80% of success in any field. Use the remaining 20% to experiment. When something works, integrate it into your foundation.
  • You are only as good as what you can get your athletes (or others) to do. - Carl Valle
  • High-performance teams often share similar tech and data. The difference comes from how they apply it and the adjustments they make.
  • In the past, I avoided hiring people I thought could be better than me, out of self-protection. This only exposed my insecurities, limited my growth, and made my position less secure in the long run.
  • Perhaps, the most important analytical skill is understanding what data CANNOT tell you. - Peter Weyand
  • When working with athletes, health is a requirement, not a goal.
  • I once took a big pay cut, and looking back, it was the best move for my future career. If you can, prioritize learning and growth over a higher paycheck.
  • I’ve worked in multiple sports without always being a technical expert. You don’t always need to have the answers. Building systems that help others find them or ask better questions is just as valuable.
  • An important skill to develop to help advance your career is the ability to come up and communicate well-founded opinions to address current challenges.
  • An easy way to gain early buy-in and credibility after joining a new organization is to find ways to help others save time on their tasks. It’s a big area of low-hanging fruit.
  • 3 keys to successful leadership: set a clear direction, empower others, and establish a continuous feedback loop.
  • Providing direction is mainly about defining the boundaries within which the members of your team can find possible solutions.
  • Regarding direction, finding the balance between setting clear boundaries and allowing space for creativity is key to let new ideas to emerge.
  • Old but relevant: Direction before speed.
  • Master the 3Cs: Code, coach, and communicate.
  • Specialization got me into pro sports; general knowledge helped me progress.
  • Athlete monitoring approaches that focus on fewer but relevant aspects will speed up learning.
  • Majority of assessments must be relevant, actionable, and can fit into training. For the ones that we can’t embedd, they must significantly impact planning or learning.
  • The more complex the environment, the simpler the solution should be. Especially in environments with heavy schedules and high volume of athletes.
  • Lift heavy often during the season is a priority. Even the busiest schedules have opportunities.
  • Meaningful experience comes from trial and error, not just years on the job. When hiring, I look for exposure to trial-and-error cycles.
  • Test, try, and apply to develop intuition. High performance requires making decisiona with limited info and high uncertainty.
  • The main limitation to predict injuries is our ability to collect meaningful data.
  • Athletes can't care less about how many championships you've won in the past. They value what you bring today.
  • Skill acquisition and player recruitment often take a backseat to routine sports science tasks like athlete monitoring. A key role for sports scientists is creating systems that help others to make decisions, freeing time to research and develop other critical areas.
  • My first jobs came through interviews. Networking and connections came into play later after showing value in previous roles. Learning to interview well is an essential skill.
  • I applied to more jobs than I remember, sometimes ones I wasn't interested in, just to get better at interviewing.
  • Advertise job vacancies as much as possible, even if you have contacts in mind for the role. You never know what talent is out there.
  • In many job interviews, I was asked about my philosophy on different topics. For me, philosophy refers to my values, beliefs and priorities, which help guide decisions and ensure consistency in my actions.
  • The ability to elaborate and explain your philosphy matters more than being "right or wrong." Breaking the field into smaller areas (e.g., speed, injuries, strength), documenting my beliefs, and review them regularly provides clarity and focus.
  • The goal is to establish good operational processes; talent, coaching and luck handle the rest.
  • A big plus for me when hiring for senior roles is at least 4 years of continuous experience in the same place. It’s an arbitrary number, but I feel this time allows for sufficient implementation, iteration, and learning.
  • A strong proof that your team is setting the standard is when your coaches become sought after by other organizations.
  • The sports world is small. I once interviewed someone who had interviewed me six years earlier. Be kind; you never know when you'll cross paths again.
  • How many problems have you solved in sports? How many solutions could become products? Think about ways to productize your work beyond your job.
  • Ask five people in your organization what 'Player X' has to improve. If there's little agreement, you've found a big opportunity.
  • When people are overpaid relative to their value, every idea, opinion, and person becomes a threat to them.
  • As I took on more leadership roles, improving how I approached difficult conversations became one of the most crucial adjustments.
  • If an organization needs a Head of Innovation; it's probably not truly innovative.
  • 'Winning Culture' is an overhyped term. You can't build culture; it's a consequence of people aligning with a purpose and finding value in the process.
  • Better data at meaningful times outweights continuous monitoring.
  • Practice design is often overlooked by sport scientists, yet it's one of the highest impact areas for coaches and athletes - I'm not talking about just handling GPS metrics.
  • Break performance down into smaller, measurable components whenever you can. Athletes are more likely to buy in when they see clear links to performance.
  • Courses and certifications come and go. Training theory and principles are ethernal.
  • As a sport scientist, one of the most lasting things I can leave behind for an organization is solid data — something that will outlast generations of employees.
  • In many fields — especially sport science — it's better to approach things with healthy skepticism rather than blind optimism.
  • Growth comes from exposing yourself to environments that require you to learn and adapt. Not through paid mentorships.
  • Affordable, reliable, and practical — you can have any two, but almost never all three at once.
