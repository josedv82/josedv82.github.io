Jose Fernandez

Born in Spain, I have a formal education in High Performance and Sport Science. I began my career at youth basketball academies, often working with fewer resources, then expanded my experience across Europe and China. After spending more than six years with the Houston Astros in MLB, I now lead performance and R&D at BCSG, a multi-club sport firm managing soccer teams across international leagues. Having worked in basketball, baseball, and football (soccer), I’ve developed a strong interest and skills in data analysis and programming along the way. I'm passionate about building and consider myself an amateur entrepreneur.



Thoughts

A Personal Take on Various Aspects of High-Performance Environments. My Perspective, Not Universal Truth.

✖
  • Master the basics—they're 80% of success in any field. Use the remaining 20% to experiment. When something works, integrate it into your foundation.
  • You are only as good as what you can get your athletes (or others) to do. - Carl Valle
  • High-performance teams often share similar tech and data. The difference comes from how they apply it and the adjustments they make.
  • In the past, I avoided hiring people I thought could be better than me, out of self-protection. This only exposed my insecurities, limited my growth, and made my position less secure in the long run.
  • Perhaps, the most important analytical skill is understanding what data CANNOT tell you. - Peter Weyand
  • When working with athletes, health is a requirement, not a goal.
  • I once took a big pay cut, and looking back, it was the best move for my future career. If you can, prioritize learning and growth over a higher paycheck.
  • I’ve worked in multiple sports without always being a technical expert. You don’t always need to have the answers. Building systems that help others find them or ask better questions is just as valuable.
  • An important skill to develop to help advance your career is the ability to come up and communicate well-founded opinions to address current challenges.
  • A good hack for gaining early buy-in and credibility after joining a new organization is to find ways to help others save time on their tasks. It’s a big area of low-hanging fruit.
  • 3 keys to successful leadership: set a clear direction, empower others, and establish a continuous feedback loop.
  • Providing direction is mainly about defining the boundaries within which the members of your team can find possible solutions.
  • With direction, finding the sweet spot between setting boundaries and allowing space for creativity is key. Great ideas can come from anywhere.
  • Old but relevant: Direction before speed.
  • Master the 3Cs: Code, coach, and communicate — Good skills in these areas can give you an immediate edge. Though, the margin is narrowing.
  • Highly specialized knowledge — being in the 99th percentile in one area — opened doors to pro sports for me. Once inside, I stayed by operating in the 80th percentile in multiple areas. Specialization got me in; but being more generalist helped me progress.
  • In athlete monitoring, I seek to simplify data collection to accelerate learning. Focus on fewer aspects at a time.
  • Choose assessments that are relevant, actionable, and can be embedded into training. For those that can't be embedded, ensure they will make a significant impact in your planning or learning.
  • The more complex the environment, the simpler the solution. With complex schedules and high volume of athletes, straighforward plans produce better results.
  • Prioritize getting athletes to lift heavy in-season. Even the busiest schedules have opportunities. Find and use them.
  • Quality experience comes from trial and error, not just years on the job. When hiring, focus on a candidate's exposure to trial-and-error cycles.
  • Trial-and-error helps build intuition. In sports, we often make decisions with incomplete information and high uncertainty. Combining trial-and-error with analytical support is essential to refine our gut feeling.
  • The main limitation to predict injuries is our ability to collect meaningful data.
  • I thought winning a championship was the peak of my career. But in a new job, those coaches and players didn't care. Your worth is defined by the value you deliver in the present.
  • Sport scientists often prioritize athlete monitoring and load management, missing higher ROI areas like skill acquisition and player recruitment. Build systems to delegate routine tasks, freeing up time to work on other high-impact areas.
  • Contacts and networking got me only one job. Every other job came through interviews. Learning to interview is a crucial skill.
  • I must have applied to nearly 100 jobs, sometimes ones I wasn't interested in, just to improve my interviewing skills.
  • Always advertise job vacancies publicly, even if you have contacts in mind. You never know what talent is out there.
  • In many job interviews, I was asked about my philosophy on various topics. Philosophy is nothing else than what you consider valuable. Your beliefs and priorities. Developing and refining your philosophy is important to help guide decisions and drive consistency.
  • Regarding philosophy, the frequency of vague explanations is more problematic than being 'right or wrong'. A good practice is to start by breaking down the field into specific areas (e.g., speed development, hamstring injury risk, in-season strength, etc), document your set of beliefs and review over time.
  • I was a staff member in multiple championship finals. Looking back, we were better prepared for the ones we lost. The goal is to establish processes that keep the team competitive; talent, coaching and luck handle the rest.
  • In S&C, sport science, or high-performance roles, it takes at least four years in one job to gain meaningful experience. This period allows for sufficient implementation, iteration, and learning.
  • A strong proof that your team is setting the standard is when your coaches become sought after by other organizations.
  • The sports world is small. I once interviewed someone who had interviewed me six years earlier. Be kind; you never know when you'll cross paths again.
  • I helped create two start-ups (quit one). How many problems have you solved in sports? How many solutions could become products? Think about ways to productize your work beyond your job.
  • Ask five people in your organization what 'Player X' has to improve. If there's little agreement, you've found a big opportunity.
  • When people are overpaid relative to their value, every idea, opinion, and person becomes a threat to them.
  • As I took on more leadership roles, learning to have difficult conversations early became one of the most crucial adjustment.
  • If an organization needs a Head of Innovation; it's probably not truly innovative.
  • 'Winning Culture' is an overhyped term. You can't build culture; it's a consequence of people aligning with a purpose and finding value in the process.
  • Better data at meaningful times outweights continuous monitoring.
  • Help coaches design better practices. Practice design is often overlooked by sport scientists, yet it's one of the highest impact areas for coaches and athletes, (I'm not talking about just handling GPS metrics).
  • Performance consultants are more common now than before but often offer less value, for various reasons including lack of 'skin in the game'. When to engage them? If they bring top 3% expertise in a technical domain, your department needs an audit, or you need to outsource routine, time-consuming tasks.
  • Break down performance into smaller, measurable components whenever possible. You'll get automatic buy-in with your athletes if you can show clear links to performance.
  • Courses and certifications come and go. Training theory and principles are ethernal.
  • As a sport scientist, the most significant legacy that I can leave an organization is data, as it will outlast generations of employees. While for coaches, the best legacy I can leave may just be a curious mindset.
  • In many fields - and especially in sport science - it's better to be skeptical than overly optimistic about most things.
  • Anyone asking for payment isn't a mentor. Growth comes from exposing yourself to environments that require you to learn and adapt.
  • Affordable, reliable, and practical — pick any two, but you can never have all three.
