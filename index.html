Brain Dump

Thoughts and takeaways from my career in High-Performance sport environments.

  • In most domains (S&C, Data Analysis, etc.) 80% is basics done well. Experiment with the other 20%. Integrate success from that 20% into the 80% over time.
  • You are only as good as what you can get your athletes (or others) to do. - Carl Valle
  • Most high-performance departments use the same technologies and collect the same data nowadays. The difference is in the quality of their interventions and their ability to adjust.
  • At times, I've been guilty of not hiring people more skilled than me. This only revealed my insecurities, limited my growth, and ultimately put my job at greater risk.
  • Perhaps, the most important analytical skill is understanding what data CANNOT tell you. - Peter Weyand
  • When working with athletes, health is a requirement, not a goal.
  • I took a big salary cut at one point in my career, and in retrospect, it was the best decision I made. If circumstances allow, consider opportunities that offer learning and growth over just higher pay.
  • If you ask me, one thing that I did well as sport scientist was not providing good answers to questions, but building systems to help others come up with better answers (and better questions).
  • The previous point does not mean opinions are not important. It's hard to advance your career if you are not able to come up with meaningful opinions to help with current problems.
  • An effective way to gain early buy-in and credibility within the first three months of joining an organization is to develop methods that help others save time on their current tasks.
  • Providing direction is mainly about defining the boundaries within which the members of your team can find possible solutions.
  • Code, coach, and communicate — the 3Cs. Mastering these areas can give you an immediate edge. Although, the margin is getting smaller.
  • Highly specialized knowledge — being in the top 3% in one area — opened the doors to pro sports for me. Once inside, I stayed by identifying bottlenecks and helping with current challenges. Specialization set me apart, but being a generalist helped me thrive.
  • When it comes to athlete monitoring, I seek to simplify data collection to speed up learning. Focus on fewer aspects at a time.
  • Prioritize getting athletes to lift heavy in-season. Even the busiest schedules have opportunities. Find those and use them.
  • I used to think experience was overrated. Now, I value it more. Quality experience gained through trial and error, over mere years on the job. When hiring, seek to learn about the candidate's exposure to trial-and-error cycles.
  • Trial-and-error helps build intuition. In sports, we make decisions with incomplete information and high uncertainty all the time. Trial-and-error coupled with analytical support is essential to improve our gut feeling.
  • The main limitation to predict injuries is our ability to collect meaningful data.
  • I thought winning the World Series was the peak of my career. Then I moved on to a new job and those coaches and players could not care less. Your status depends only on the value you can deliver to them.
  • Sport scientists often focus primarily on athlete monitoring and load management, overlooking higher ROI areas like skill acquisition and player recruitment. Instead, build a system to delegate routine tasks, freeing up time for high-impact areas.
  • Contacts and networking only got me one job. Every other job came through interviews. Learning to interview is a crucial skill.
  • I've probably applied to nearly 100 jobs over the years, sometimes even ones I wasn't interested in, just to improve my interviewing skills. 100% of those interviews were outside of my home country (a topic for another day).
  • Always advertise job vacancies publicly. Even if you have contacts in mind. You never know what talent is out there.
  • I was a staff member in three World Series. Looking back, we were better prepared when we lost than when we won. The goal is to establish processes that keep the team competitive; the rest is down to talent, coaching and luck.
  • For S&C, sport science, or high-performance roles, I believe it takes a minimum of four years at one job to gain valuable experience. This time frame allows for enough implementation, iteration, and learning.
  • I once interviewed someone who had interviewed me six years earlier. The sports world is very small. Be kind to others.
  • I helped create two start-ups (I quit one). How many problems have you solved in sports? How many of those solutions could become a product? Think about ways to productize your work outside your work.
  • If you don't know where to start, ask five people in your organization what 'Player X' has to improve. If you find little consensus, that's a massive opportunity.
  • When people are overpaid relative to their value, every idea, opinion, and person becomes a threat to them.
  • As I took on more leadership roles, learning to have difficult conversations early in the process became one of the most important adjustments I had to make.
  • An organization that needs a Head of Innovation is probably not truly innovative.
  • Winning culture is a hyped term. You can't build culture; it's a consequence of people aligning with a purpose and finding value in the process.
  • Meaningful data at meaningful times is more valuable than continuous monitoring.
  • In one of my first jobs I sat at the coaches' table for breakfast. The head coach asked, 'How can you help us?' I said something about 'data this...data that...'. Today, my answer would be 'I'll help you design better practices.' Practice design is often overlooked by sport scientists when it actually is one of the highest impact areas with coaches, and indirectly, athletes too. I am not just referring to handling GPS metrics here.
  • There are more 'performance consultants' now than 15 years ago but it is more difficult to get value from consultants now than 15 years ago.
  • When to work with consultants: 1) they bring top 3% knowledge in a specific (technical) domain, 2) a department running on autopilot that can benefit from an audit and, 3) outsource routine, time consuming, low quality tasks.
