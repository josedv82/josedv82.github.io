Random Reflections

Ongoing list with my thoughts, opinions and takeaways from working in High-Performance sporting environments.

  • In most domains (S&C, Data Analysis, etc.) 80% is basics done well. Experiment with the other 20%. Integrate success from that 20% into the 80% over time.
  • You are only as good as what you can get your athletes (or others) to do. - Carl Valle
  • Most high-performance departments use the same technologies and collect the same data nowadays. The difference is in what adjustments you can make.
  • I've been guilty of not hiring people more skilled than me. This only revealed my insecurities, limited my growth, and ultimately put my job at greater risk.
  • Perhaps, the most important analytical skill is understanding what data CANNOT tell you. - Peter Weyand
  • When working with athletes, health is a requirement, not a goal.
  • I once took a significant salary cut, and in retrospect, it was the best decision I made. If circumstances allow, consider opportunities for learning and growth over higher pay.
  • As a sport scientist, my strength wasn’t in providing good answers, but in creating systems that helped others find better answers and ask better questions.
  • Systems are key, but having meaningful opinions is essential. To advance your career, you need to be able to provide meaningful opinions to help with current issues.
  • An effective way to gain early buy-in and credibility within the first three months of joining an organization is to develop methods that help others save time on their current tasks.
  • Providing direction is mainly about defining the boundaries within which the members of your team can find possible solutions.
  • Master the 3Cs: Code, coach, and communicate — Excelling in these areas can give you an immediate edge. Though, the margin is narrowing.
  • Highly specialized knowledge — being in the top 3% in one area — opened doors to pro sports for me. Once inside, I stayed by identifying bottlenecks and solving current challenges. Specialization got me in; being a generalist helped me progress.
  • In athlete monitoring, I seek to simplify data collection to accelerate learning. Focus on fewer aspects at a time.
  • The more complex the environment, the simpler the solution. With complex schedules and high volume of athletes, straighforward plans produce better results.
  • Prioritize getting athletes to lift heavy in-season. Even the busiest schedules have opportunities. Find and use them.
  • Quality experience comes from trial and error, not just years on the job. When hiring, focus on a candidate's exposure to trial-and-error cycles.
  • Trial-and-error helps build intuition. In sports, we often make decisions with incomplete information and high uncertainty. Combining trial-and-error with analytical support is essential to refine our gut feeling.
  • The main limitation to predict injuries is our ability to collect meaningful data.
  • I thought winning the World Series was the peak of my career. But in a new job, those coaches and players didn't care. Your worth is defined by the value you deliver in the present.
  • Sport scientists often prioritize athlete monitoring and load management, missing higher ROI areas like skill acquisition and player recruitment. Build systems to delegate routine tasks, freeing up time to work on other high-impact areas.
  • Contacts and networking got me only one job. Every other job came through interviews. Learning to interview is a crucial skill.
  • I must have applied to nearly 100 jobs, sometimes ones I wasn't interested in, just to improve my interviewing skills.
  • Always advertise job vacancies publicly, even if you have contacts in mind. You never know what talent is out there.
  • In many job interviews, I was asked about my philosophy on various topics. Philosophy is nothing else than what you consider valuable. Your beliefs and priorities. Developing and refining your philosophy is important to help guide decisions and drive consistency.
  • Regarding philosophy, the frequency of vague explanations is more problematic than being 'right or wrong'. I always recommend starting by breaking down the field into specific areas (e.g., speed development, hamstring injury risk, in-season strength, etc), document your set of beliefs and review over time.
  • I was a staff member in three World Series. Looking back, we were better prepared for the ones we lost. The goal is to establish processes that keep the team competitive; talent, coaching and luck handle the rest.
  • In S&C, sport science, or high-performance roles, it takes at least four years in one job to gain valuable experience. This period allows for sufficient implementation, iteration, and learning.
  • The sports world is small. I once interviewed someone who had interviewed me six years earlier. Be kind; you never know when you'll cross paths again.
  • I helped create two start-ups (quit one). How many problems have you solved in sports? How many solutions could become products? Think about ways to productize your work beyond your job.
  • Ask five people in your organization what 'Player X' has to improve. If there's little agreement, you've found a big opportunity.
  • When people are overpaid relative to their value, every idea, opinion, and person becomes a threat to them.
  • As I took on more leadership roles, learning to have difficult conversations early became one of the most crucial adjustments.
  • If an organization needs a Head of Innovation; it's probably not truly innovative.
  • 'Winning Culture' is an overhyped term. You can't build culture; it's a consequence of people aligning with a purpose and finding value in the process.
  • Better data at meaningful times outweights continuous monitoring.
  • Help coaches design better practices. Practice design is often overlooked by sport scientists, yet it's one of the highest impact areas for coaches and athletes, (I'm not talking about just handling GPS metrics).
  • Performance consultants are more common now than before but often offer less value, for various reasons including lack of 'skin in the game'. When to engage them? If they bring top 3% expertise in a technical domain, your department needs an audit, or you need to outsource routine, time-consuming tasks.
  • Break down performance into smaller, measurable components whenever possible. You'll get automatic buy-in with your athletes if you can show clear links to performance.
  • Courses and certifications come and go. Training theory and principles are ethernal.
  • As a sport scientist, the most significant legacy you can leave an organization is data, as it will outlast generations of employees. For a coach, the best legacy may just be a curious mindset.
  • In most domains, and especially in sport science, it's better to be skeptical than overly optimistic about new technologies or program additions.
